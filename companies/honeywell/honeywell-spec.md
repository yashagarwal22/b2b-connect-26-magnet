## 0. The spine (read this first)

Everything below hangs on one insight about Honeywell's situation, drawn straight from the Account 360.

**Honeywell is being taken apart, and that is the cleanest data-foundation moment it will ever get.** Solstice Advanced Materials already spun off in October 2025. Aerospace Technologies follows in Q3 2026. The company has run as four segments since Q1 FY2026. Every one of those separated businesses must stand up its own commerce, CRM and marketing data, on a deadline.

That is either four new messes or one clean foundation. A breakup forces a rebuild of exactly the customer and product data that AI needs to be true. Do it in a hurry, segment by segment, and each new company inherits the same fragmentation Honeywell has lived with for decades. Govern it once, as the instances separate, and the separation pays a dividend no normal year could.

The buyer in the room is Alesia Miller, Global Sr. Marketing Director, whose remit is customer acquisition, CRM, data analytics, CX and digital strategy. Her question is not "should we add AI." It is whether the separation hands her a clean customer-data foundation or four fragmented ones, and whether AI in her remit ever gets something true to run on.

So the strategic question for Honeywell is simple. Does the once-in-a-century separation become four prettier marketing stacks, or the moment the customer and product data finally gets governed right. For a company whose aftermarket and installed base are the profit engine, the answer decides whether AI in marketing and commerce ever pays.

That is where AI actually pays here. Not a Forge copilot on every screen. Not a generic chatbot. It is being cited by the AI engines buyers now use, account-aware aftermarket self-service, and the governed customer-and-product data underneath both. The model is the commodity. A century of installed-base, aftermarket and specification data, and the buyer relationships Siemens and Emerson cannot copy, are the moat.

That spine runs through the thesis, the wedge, every module, and every play. Guardrail: the ERP core is SAP and a prime SI is running the separation. i95Dev enters complementary, on the customer-data-to-commerce seam for one segment's aftermarket, never the SAP core.

```

  ●               ○                  ○                  ○
```

Make a named AI engine cite Honeywell over a distributor on one product family, and stand up account-based parts self-service for that segment. It runs on owned spec and contract data and proves the thesis fast. This is the wedge.

**Phase 2, 3 to 6 months. RAG spec lookup and PIM enrichment.**
Spec-grounded answers on the segment catalog, plus AI PIM enrichment so every SKU is searchable and AEO-ready. The Grainger pattern, scoped to one business.

**Phase 3, 6 to 12 months. Customer data unification per business.**
Unify Adobe and Salesforce data into one profile per separated company, so campaigns and CX run on real behavior, not list sends.

**Phase 4, the foundation. Governed master data as the instances separate.**
Govern customer and product master data once, the moment each SAP instance stands up, alongside the prime SI. The work begins quietly in Phase 1 and matures here.

*Why this order.* The fast, owned-data wins go first and self-fund the deeper foundation work, and the foundation rides the separation timeline rather than fighting it.
