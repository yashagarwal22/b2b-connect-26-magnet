## 0. The spine (read this first)

Everything below hangs on one insight about Masimo's situation, drawn straight from the Account 360.

**Masimo's advantage is the data under a razor-and-blade healthcare business, and a pending acquisition means the only fundable move is the one that runs on that data and survives the close.** It is an 80%-plus recurring, 98%-renewal sensor business, now a focused healthcare company after divesting Sound United. But it has agreed to be acquired by Danaher for about $9.9B, with the close expected in the second half of 2026. Danaher will bring its own systems and the Danaher Business System.

That reframes everything for the buyer, Apoorva Jayaram, Director of Strategic Marketing, who controls up to about $250k. A pending acquisition typically freezes net-new IT and marketing spend, and anything bought net-new now risks being ripped out at close. The honest counsel is not to push a platform. It is to ship one small thing that pays now and travels into the new org.

The asset that travels is the first-party data. Who reorders, how often, which hospital, which protocol, on a 98%-renewal base. Activating that inside the budget Jayaram already controls is the rare spend that survives an acquisition, and it can become a reference inside the Danaher portfolio, which already includes IDT, a higher-fit target.

So the strategic question for Masimo is not whether to transform. It is which single, owned-data win is worth doing in a freeze. For a company about to change systems, the answer is the work that depends on no new platform.

That is where AI actually pays here. Not a net-new martech platform mid-deal. Not a chatbot on an informational site. It is first-party reorder and account data, activated inside $250k. The model is the commodity. The installed-base data on a 98%-renewal business is the moat.

That spine runs through the thesis, the wedge, every module, and every play. Guardrail: nurture, do not push; the only safe spend runs on data Masimo already owns.
