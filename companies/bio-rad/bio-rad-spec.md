## 0. The spine (read this first)

Everything below hangs on one insight about Bio-Rad, drawn straight from the Account 360.

**Bio-Rad is digitizing its customer touchpoints with a CDP, and the CDP will only pay if the data underneath it is unified first.** Customer interactions are moving digital, and Harmeet Arora, Head of Marketing Analytics, is driving a CDP implementation plus marketing automation. At the same time Bio-Rad is mid-SAP-transformation, with Syniti running the data migration and Kinaxis modernizing supply chain. The foundation work is already underway.

The trap is sequence. A CDP unifies whatever you feed it, gaps included, and 53% of failed AI initiatives blame data quality, not the model. Standing up activation features before the first-party data is unified means targeting partially and modeling churn with holes in it. The order matters: unify the data, then light up the CDP.

There is a real constraint to name. Harmeet Arora is a champion but holds no personal budget, so this is an influencer relationship that must multi-thread to the economic buyer, the CMO, CDO or CIO, to get funded.

So the strategic question for Bio-Rad is whether the CDP becomes a clean source of truth or an expensive layer over fragmented data. For a company already paying for an SAP and Syniti cleanup, the smart move is to make that the same foundation the CDP draws from.

That is where AI actually pays here. Not a CDP switched on over silos. Not generic outbound content. It is first-party data unified on the SAP and Syniti foundation, a CDP fed by it, and demand sensing on the reagent business. The model is the commodity. The direct relationship with the labs, hospitals and researchers who reorder Bio-Rad's reagents and controls, which Thermo Fisher and Agilent cannot copy, is the moat.

That spine runs through the thesis, the wedge, every module, and every play. Guardrail: keep the first scope modest given no attendee budget, and co-sell to the economic buyer.
