## 0. The spine (read this first)

Everything below hangs on one insight about CP Skin's business, drawn straight from the Account 360.

**CP Skin does not win on advertising. It wins in the chair.** EltaMD is the number-one dermatologist-recommended sunscreen. PCA SKIN is the number-one professional chemical peel. These brands were not built by a media auction. They were built by the dermatologist, the plastic surgeon and the aesthetician who hand them to a patient and say "use this." That professional recommendation is the moat. SkinCeuticals and AI can copy a landing page. They cannot copy the chair.

Then, in Q4 2025, Colgate took a $794M impairment on the skin-health business. Overnight, digital growth became a board-level mandate.

Here is the trap that mandate sets. Under impairment scrutiny, the reflex is to "win back share," and every agency in the room will sell CP Skin more performance media and an AI conversion uplift, that is, marketing to strangers in the same Google and Meta auction L'Oréal will always outbid. The research is blunt on why that fails: acquisition is the worst-ROI growth there is, and it costs five to seven times more than keeping a customer you already have.

For a brand built on professional trust, the customers you already have are the richest asset in the building. They are the patients a professional sent you, and the professionals who sent them. That is first-party data CP Skin owns, that SkinCeuticals cannot see, and that ordinary AI can compound: the next refill, the regimen cross-sell, the practice that reorders.

So the strategic question is not "how do we win back digital traffic." It is "how do we grow the patients and professionals we already earned, faster than L'Oréal can buy new ones." Answer that, and the impairment becomes the reason the board funds the cheapest growth CP Skin has. Miss it, and the $2M+ goes into an auction the brand is structurally built to lose.

That spine runs through the thesis, the wedge, every module, and every play.
