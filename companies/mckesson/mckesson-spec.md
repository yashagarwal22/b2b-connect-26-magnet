## 0. The spine (read this first)

Everything below hangs on one insight about McKesson's business, drawn straight from the Account 360.

**McKesson does not win on product. It wins by being the most reliable, most intelligent distribution network in healthcare, at a margin so thin that a single point of efficiency is enormous.** It moves a third of the pharmaceuticals in North America, then layers higher-margin specialty, oncology and biopharma services on top. The moat is not the drugs. It is the network, the contracts, and the data that runs them.

This is not a company that needs to be told to do AI. McKesson already treats AI as core infrastructure: prior-authorization automation, supply forecasting, disruption prediction, fraud detection, and AI plus robotics in its distribution centers. It was named a Fortune Most Innovative Company in 2025. The big programs are already owned, internally and with hyperscalers and global SIs.

So the strategic question for a partner like i95Dev is not "should McKesson do AI." It is far narrower and more honest. At roughly $359B in revenue, with Accenture-class firms and internal teams running the platform programs, where can a surgical, commerce-and-ERP-data-deep specialist add a win the giants are structurally built not to scope. The answer is one bounded, compliant-by-design workflow, run on McKesson's own data, sized to the $2M Jennifer Lai controls, with an audited number at the end.

That is where AI actually pays for a specialist here. Not a seven-platform transformation. Not a copilot on every screen. One regulated, high-value workflow, prior-authorization and serialization document intelligence, specialty demand sensing, supplier-network risk, resolved and measured. The model is the commodity. McKesson's proprietary distribution and oncology data, and the regulated workflows Cencora and Cardinal cannot copy, are the moat.

That spine runs through the thesis, the wedge, every module, and every play. The single most important guardrail: stay narrow, stay compliant, prove a number, and never pretend a niche vendor can or should run McKesson's enterprise AI agenda.

## 10. The money pools (where the dollars are)

**Device:** four horizontal bars, each with one named proof point. Headline $3–12M. The four pools are the four levers of a regulated distributor: automate the compliant paperwork, free the working capital, insure the network, and give back the service time.

```
 Compliance & document-AI: prior-auth and serialization, touchless   $1M ████████████ $4M
 Working capital: demand sensing on specialty and oncology           $1M ██████████████ $5M
 Resilience: supplier-network risk, disruption avoided               $0.5M ████████ $2M
 Service time: order exceptions resolved without a rep               $0.5M ████████ $1M
                                                                      └─ headline: $3–12M ─┘
```

**Pool 1. Compliance and document-AI: prior-auth and serialization, touchless. ($1–4M)**
McKesson already automates prior-auth; the next gain is touchless document intelligence across regulated paperwork. FRoSTA reached 60% touchless invoice processing with SAP Document AI at under a minute per document. The same compliant, human-in-the-loop pattern applied to prior-auth and serialization exceptions converts manual review into capacity, auditable end to end. *(Verified. Source: SAP / FRoSTA; McKesson prior-auth, Account 360.)*

**Pool 2. Working capital: demand sensing on specialty and oncology. ($1–5M)**
Specialty and oncology products are high-value and time-sensitive, so expiry and stockout both cost. Demand sensing on McKesson's own order history reduces expiry write-offs and frees working capital; the life-sciences analog is a roughly 30% cut in expiry write-offs, and BRF's SAP IBP demand sensing cut planning time 33%. At McKesson scale, single-digit-percent inventory improvement is large. *(Verified. Source: BRF / SAP IBP; research dump life-sciences example.)*

**Pool 3. Resilience: supplier-network risk, disruption avoided. ($0.5–2M)**
A single supply disruption can interrupt drug availability to providers and patients. Multi-tier risk monitoring gives 60 to 90 day advance warnings; a C3.ai HealthTech manufacturer cut delayed sales orders 35% in four weeks with supply-network risk. Avoiding one or two events a year is asymmetric insurance on a network this critical. *(Verified / vendor-claimed. Source: Resilinc / Everstream; C3.ai.)*

**Pool 4. Service time: order exceptions resolved without a rep. ($0.5–1M)**
Where McKesson's service desks hand-navigate exceptions, an AI layer deflects the routine. Fisher & Paykel moved self-service from 40% to 70% and saved 3,300 hours a month; JPW Industries cut order processing from 16 to 24 hours to under 1 hour. The same pattern converts service time into capacity without headcount. *(Fisher & Paykel verified; JPW vendor-corroborated.)*

*Why four.* They are the four levers of a regulated distributor: automate the compliant paperwork, free the working capital, insure the network, and give back the service time. Each is a bounded workflow, not a program, and each maps to a phase and a play.

### Operations (the compliant core)

- *ROI:* Touchless, human-in-the-loop document handling converts manual review into capacity with a full audit trail.
- *What i95Dev does:* Build one bounded document-AI workflow, compliant-by-design and human-in-the-loop, with a baseline and an audited result, inside the $2M. *(Verified. SAP / FRoSTA.)*

**B. Demand sensing for specialty and oncology.**
- *Problem:* High-value, time-sensitive products mean both expiry and stockout cost real money, and classic forecasting misses the signal until it is too late.
- *Named case:* BRF's SAP IBP demand sensing cut planning time 33%; the life-sciences pattern cuts expiry write-offs by roughly 30%.
- *ROI:* Single-digit-percent inventory improvement frees significant working capital at McKesson scale.
- *What i95Dev does:* Layer demand sensing on McKesson's own order and contract history for one specialty line, then widen. *(Verified. BRF / SAP IBP.)*

**C. Multi-tier supplier and network risk.**
- *Problem:* One disruption can interrupt drug availability to providers; tier-2 and tier-3 visibility is thin across most networks.
- *Named case:* Resilinc and Everstream give 60 to 90 day advance warnings; a C3.ai HealthTech manufacturer cut delayed sales orders 35% in four weeks.
- *ROI:* Avoiding one or two events a year is asymmetric insurance on a critical network.
- *What i95Dev does:* Add supplier-network risk signals to McKesson's planning and order data so disruption is visible before it reaches a provider. *(Verified / vendor-claimed. Resilinc / Everstream; C3.ai.)*

### Service

**D. Order-exception and service automation.**
- *Problem:* Service desks hand-navigate routine exceptions, which is time that could be capacity.
- *Named case:* Fisher & Paykel moved self-service 40% to 70% and saved 3,300 hours a month; JPW Industries cut order processing from 16 to 24 hours to under 1 hour.
- *ROI:* Deflecting routine exceptions converts service hours into capacity without headcount.
- *What i95Dev does:* An AI layer over the service workflow that resolves common cases and routes only true exceptions to a person. *(Fisher & Paykel verified; JPW vendor-corroborated.)*

### The foundation

**E. The ERP-to-data integration seam.**
- *Problem:* The warehouse, ERP, commerce and service systems each hold part of the truth, and 87% of leaders say bad data has hampered AI value. Every play above runs on this.
- *Named case:* Stibo and Informatica are Gartner MDM Leaders; Palantir's Eaton work shows the payoff of automating the ERP-data seam.

- *What i95Dev does:* A surgical integration and data layer for the specific workflow in scope, alongside the incumbent SIs, never replacing them. *(Illustrative. Stibo / Informatica; Palantir / Eaton.)*
